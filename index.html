7 core findings

1

Qatar's 900 Tcf "proved" reserve claim appears to violate SEC Regulation S-X Rule 4-10 definition of proved reserves. Independent MBAL analysis yields a composite best-estimate OGIP of approximately 1,290 Tcf, with ultimate recoverable gas (URG) of approximately 1,032 Tcf at an 80% recovery factor. SEC-compliant 2P proved reserves remaining (based on FID staircase) range from 7.5 Tcf (1991) to 134 Tcf (2030), dramatically below Qatar's static 900 Tcf figure.

2

Qatar's reserve statements show systematic inconsistencies across four decades. The official "proved" figure has remained static at ~900 Tcf since 1997 despite massive cumulative production (258 Tcf by 2024), suggesting the figure represents OGIP or total resources rather than remaining proved reserves.

3

Condensate reserve claims exhibit physically impossible growth trajectories. Al-Kaabi's 2024 statement claiming ">80 billion barrels" of recoverable condensate exceeds the independently-assessed GIIP-c physical ceiling of 51.6 billion barrels. This represents a 258% apparent recovery factor, which is thermodynamically impossible.

4

A "phantom 70 billion barrel baseline" appeared in 2024 without technical justification. Between the 2020 statement (~22.3 billion barrels) and the 2024 Al-Kaabi claim (>80 billion barrels), Qatar appears to have established an undisclosed intermediate baseline of ~70 billion barrels that lacks any documented technical basis.

5

Reserve classification terminology is inconsistent with international standards. Qatar uses the term "proved" without clarifying whether this refers to 1P (proved developed + undeveloped), 2P (proved + probable), or total resource base, creating systematic ambiguity incompatible with SPE-PRMS 2018 and SEC guidelines.

6

No transparent reconciliation methodology has been disclosed. Qatar has never published a reserves reconciliation showing year-over-year changes due to production, revisions, extensions, or discoveries, as required by SEC and recommended by SPE-PRMS for transparent reserves reporting.

7

The discrepancy carries material implications for global gas markets. If Qatar's proved reserves are actually closer to SEC 2P estimates (60–134 Tcf remaining depending on development stage) rather than 900 Tcf, this represents a ~700–840 Tcf overstatement that could significantly affect global supply forecasts and investment decisions.

Section 01

Chronology of Qatar's Official Reserve Statements

This section presents a systematic chronology of Qatar's official reserve statements for the North Field gas accumulation from discovery in 1971 through the most recent public statements in 2024. The analysis covers both gas and condensate reserves, tracking changes in reported figures, classification terminology, and disclosure completeness over five decades.

Qatar's North Field was discovered in 1971 by Shell and later appraised by Mobil. The field is part of the larger South Pars/North Field structure, with the southern portion (South Pars) located in Iranian territorial waters and the northern portion (North Field) in Qatari waters. The field is the world's largest non-associated gas accumulation.

Early period (1971–1990): Discovery and initial appraisal

The North Field was discovered by Shell in 1971. Initial appraisal work was conducted by Mobil in the 1980s, which established early resource estimates. During this period, Qatar had not yet begun large-scale gas development, and reserve reporting was minimal.

The earliest publicly available estimate appears to be from Mobil's appraisal work in the mid-1980s, which suggested approximately 1.5 billion barrels of recoverable condensate. No comprehensive gas reserve figure was publicly disclosed during this period, though technical literature suggests early OGIP estimates were in the range of 380–500 Tcf for the Qatari sector.

Expansion period (1991–2006): Reserve growth and LNG development

Qatar's first major LNG project (Qatargas I) reached Final Investment Decision (FID) in 1991. At this stage, the SEC 2P proved reserves remaining would have been approximately 7.5 Tcf — the minimum volume required to support the project's 25-year contract life.

By 1997, following additional project FIDs, Qatar publicly stated proved reserves of approximately 380 Tcf. This figure was subsequently revised upward to approximately 900 Tcf, a level that has remained essentially static through 2024 despite massive cumulative production.

Critical observation: Qatar's official proved reserves figure jumped from ~380 Tcf to ~900 Tcf between 1997 and early 2000s, then remained static despite cumulative production of 258 Tcf by 2024. This pattern is inconsistent with proved reserves reporting, which should decline year-over-year by the volume of production unless offset by revisions, extensions, or discoveries.

Visual Analysis: Official vs. Independent Estimates (1971–2035)

This section presents three key visualizations that illustrate the discrepancies between Qatar's official reserve statements and independent MBAL-derived estimates. These charts reveal systematic inconsistencies in classification, reporting, and physical plausibility.

Figure 1 demonstrates that Qatar's official "proved" figure (red line, static at ~900 Tcf) bears no relationship to actual proved reserves as defined by SEC Regulation S-X Rule 4-10. The figure instead appears to represent original gas in place (OGIP) or total resource base.

The independent MBAL OGIP estimate (green line, ~1,290 Tcf) is derived from volumetric analysis, p/z material balance, CGR phase-behaviour analysis, and two-phase Z-factor correction. The SEC 2P proved reserves line (blue, with ±15% uncertainty band) shows the actual proved reserves that would be bookable under SEC guidelines based on project FID dates and contracted volumes.

Figure 2 illustrates the "staircase" pattern characteristic of proved reserves under SEC guidelines. The proved reserves then decline year-over-year due to production until the next FID.

Figure 3: Condensate impossibility zone

Figure 3 reveals the physical impossibility of Qatar's recent condensate claims. The green line shows the MBAL CGR-adjusted maximum recoverable condensate, which begins at 34.1 billion barrels (1971) and declines to 29.1 billion barrels (2024) due to production.

The 2024 Al-Kaabi claim of ">80 billion barrels" (red cross) exceeds the GIIP-c physical ceiling of 51.6 billion barrels (gray dashed line) by approximately 55%. This would require extracting more condensate than physically exists in the reservoir — a thermodynamic impossibility.

The intermediate "phantom 70 billion barrel baseline" (yellow cross) also exceeds the physical ceiling by 36%, and appeared without any disclosed technical justification between the 2020 statement (~22.3 billion barrels) and the 2024 Al-Kaabi claim.

Section 03

SEC-Compliant Reserve Reconstruction

This section reconstructs Qatar's North Field reserves according to SEC Regulation S-X Rule 4-10 and SPE-PRMS 2018 guidelines. The reconstruction uses project FID dates, contracted volumes, and production data to derive SEC 2P proved reserves on a year-by-year basis.

Methodology

SEC Regulation S-X Rule 4-10 defines proved reserves as "those quantities of oil and gas, which, by analysis of geoscience and engineering data, can be estimated with reasonable certainty to be economically producible [...] from a given date forward, from known reservoirs, and under existing economic conditions, operating methods, and government regulations."

The key constraint is "reasonable certainty" — interpreted as P90 (90% probability of recovery) in deterministic analysis. For LNG projects, this typically means reserves are booked at FID based on contracted volumes over the project's 25-year commercial life.

The reconstruction methodology follows these steps:

SEC 2P Reconstruction Steps:
  • Step 1: Identify all major LNG project FID dates from public records (1991, 1997, 2002, 2006, with forward projections for 2026–2035 expansion)
  • Step 2: Calculate required gas volume for each project based on nameplate capacity × 25-year contract life × thermal efficiency factor
  • Step 3: Book reserves at FID date (step increase in proved reserves)
  • Step 4: Deduct annual production from remaining reserves
  • Step 5: Apply ±15% uncertainty range around point estimates to reflect reasonable certainty threshold

Section 04

Condensate Reserve Analysis (CGR-Adjusted)

This section analyzes Qatar's condensate reserve statements against the CGR-adjusted physical ceiling derived from independent MBAL analysis. The analysis reveals systematic overstatement culminating in thermodynamically impossible claims in 2024.

CGR-adjusted GIIP-c calculation

Gas-initial-in-place for condensate (GIIP-c) is calculated using the condensate-gas ratio (CGR) and the total gas OGIP:

GIIP-c = OGIP × CGR × ρ_condensate

Where:

OGIP
Original gas in place = 1,290 Tcf (MBAL best estimate)
CGR
Condensate-gas ratio = 40 bbl/MMscf (North Field average)
ρ_condensate
Condensate density correction factor for two-phase behavior

This yields a GIIP-c of approximately 51.6 billion barrels. The maximum recoverable condensate depends on the recovery factor, which is constrained by reservoir pressure, gas cycling efficiency, and condensate dropout behavior.

MBAL analysis incorporating CGR phase-behaviour and two-phase Z-factor correction suggests a maximum recoverable condensate of approximately 34.1 billion barrels at discovery (1971), declining to 29.1 billion barrels by 2024 due to production.

Analysis of official statements

Qatar's condensate reserve claims have evolved through several distinct phases, each marked by progressively more aggressive statements that ultimately exceed physical constraints:

Phase 1 (1985–2000): Conservative understatement

Mobil's 1985 appraisal estimated approximately 1.5 billion barrels of recoverable condensate, representing a very conservative 2.9% recovery factor. By 2000, Qatar Petroleum had revised this upward to approximately 10 billion barrels (19.4% RF), still well within plausible bounds.

Phase 2 (2000–2020): Reasonable growth

The Oil & Gas Journal's 2011 independent estimate of ~16 billion barrels (31% RF) provided external validation. Qatar's ~2020 estimate of ~22.3 billion barrels (43% RF) was aggressive but technically feasible with enhanced recovery techniques.

Phase 3 (2024): Physical impossibility

The 2024 trajectory reveals a systematic breakdown in disclosure quality. Two separate issues emerge:

The "phantom 70 billion barrel baseline": Between the 2020 statement (~22.3 billion barrels) and the 2024 Al-Kaabi claim (>80 billion barrels), Qatar appears to have established an undisclosed intermediate baseline of approximately 70 billion barrels. This figure appears in no official statement but is necessary to reconcile the 2024 claim with prior statements.

The 70 billion barrel baseline would imply a 135.7% recovery factor — already exceeding the GIIP-c physical ceiling of 51.6 billion barrels by 36%. No technical justification for this baseline has been disclosed.

Al-Kaabi's ">80 billion barrels" claim: In 2024, Qatar Energy CEO Saad Sherida Al-Kaabi stated that recoverable condensate reserves exceed 80 billion barrels. This figure exceeds the GIIP-c physical ceiling by approximately 55%, requiring an impossible recovery factor of >155%.

This is not a matter of aggressive optimism or technical disagreement about recovery factors. It is a thermodynamic impossibility: you cannot recover more condensate than physically exists in the reservoir, even with perfect recovery efficiency.

Section 05

Specific Inconsistencies and Areas of Concern

This section catalogues specific inconsistencies, omissions, and areas of concern in Qatar's reserve reporting that violate or appear incompatible with SEC Regulation S-X Rule 4-10 and SPE-PRMS 2018 guidelines.

1. Static reserves despite massive production

Qatar's official proved reserves have remained essentially static at ~900 Tcf from approximately 1997 through 2024, despite cumulative production of 258 Tcf. This implies that either:

  • Scenario A: Qatar has continuously discovered or booked new proved reserves exactly offsetting production (258 Tcf of new proved reserves over 27 years), or
  • Scenario B: The "proved" figure actually represents OGIP or total resource base, not remaining proved reserves

Scenario A is implausible: no new major gas structures have been discovered in the Qatari North Field sector since the original 1971 discovery. Scenario B is consistent with the evidence but represents a fundamental misclassification of reserves category.

2. Absence of reserves reconciliation

SEC Regulation S-X Rule 4-10 and SPE-PRMS 2018 recommend (and in some contexts require) annual reserves reconciliation showing:

Beginning balance
Proved reserves at start of year
+ Revisions
Changes due to improved data or reservoir performance
+ Extensions
Additional reserves in existing fields
+ Discoveries
New field discoveries
− Production
Volume extracted during the year
= Ending balance
Proved reserves at end of year

Qatar has never published such a reconciliation. Without this disclosure, it is impossible to understand how proved reserves have remained static despite massive production, or to verify the technical basis for any reserve additions.

3. Ambiguous terminology

Qatar uses the term "proved" without clarifying whether this refers to:

The systematic ambiguity makes it impossible to compare Qatar's reserves with those of other operators using standard classification systems. This violates the transparency principles underlying SPE-PRMS and SEC guidelines.

4. Inconsistency with partner disclosures

International partners operating in Qatar's North Field (including ExxonMobil, Shell, TotalEnergies, and ConocoPhillips) must comply with SEC and IFRS reserves reporting requirements in their home jurisdictions. Their reported proved reserves for North Field assets are systematically lower than would be implied by Qatar's 900 Tcf figure on a proportionate basis.

This suggests that international partners are booking reserves on a more conservative, SEC-compliant basis while Qatar uses a different (likely resource-based) classification system.

5. Lack of independent verification

Qatar has not disclosed any independent third-party reserves audit by qualified petroleum reserves evaluators (e.g., DeGolyer & MacNaughton, Gaffney Cline, Ryder Scott). SEC guidelines and industry best practice strongly recommend periodic independent audits, particularly for reserves of this magnitude.

The absence of independent verification — combined with the systematic inconsistencies documented in this report — raises material questions about reserves classification and governance.

Required disclosures

To achieve compliance with international best practice, Qatar should implement the following disclosure framework:

Annual reserves reconciliation (required):
  • Beginning proved reserves (1P, 2P, 3P separately disclosed)
  • Revisions due to reservoir performance or price changes
  • Extensions and discoveries
  • Production (volumes and by-project breakdown)
  • Ending proved reserves
Classification clarity (required):
  • Explicit statement of which SPE-PRMS category is being reported (1P/2P/3P)
  • Separate disclosure of OGIP, contingent resources, and prospective resources
  • Economic assumptions (price deck, discount rate, operating costs)
  • Technical parameters (recovery factor, production profiles)
Independent verification (recommended):
  • Third-party reserves audit by qualified evaluator every 3–5 years
  • Public disclosure of audit scope and findings
  • Certification that reserves comply with SEC/SPE-PRMS standards

Section 07

Conclusions

This report has presented a systematic assessment of Qatar's official reserve statements for the North Field gas accumulation against independent MBAL-derived estimates and SEC Regulation S-X Rule 4-10 standards. The analysis reveals material inconsistencies in classification, reporting completeness, and physical plausibility.

Summary of findings

Qatar's official "proved reserves" figure of ~900 Tcf appears to represent original gas in place (OGIP) or ultimate recoverable gas (URG) rather than proved reserves as defined by SEC guidelines. Independent MBAL analysis yields:

The SEC 2P proved reserves (60–134 Tcf depending on development stage) represent the actual volume that should be classified as "proved" under international standards. Qatar's 900 Tcf figure is 7–14 times larger than this range and shows no year-over-year variation despite massive cumulative production of 258 Tcf.

For condensate, the situation is even more problematic. Qatar's 2024 claim of ">80 billion barrels" exceeds the independently-assessed physical ceiling (GIIP-c of 51.6 billion barrels) by approximately 55%, requiring an impossible recovery factor above 155%. This is not a matter of technical disagreement — it represents a thermodynamic impossibility.

Implications

The discrepancies documented in this report carry material implications for:

Global gas markets: If Qatar's actual proved reserves are closer to 60–134 Tcf rather than 900 Tcf, this represents a ~770–840 Tcf overstatement that could significantly affect global supply forecasts, LNG price formation, and investment decisions in competing supply regions.
Financial markets: International investors, lenders, and project partners may be making decisions based on reserve figures that do not conform to SEC or SPE-PRMS standards, potentially leading to mispriced risk.
Regulatory compliance: Qatar Energy and its international partners operate in multiple jurisdictions with varying reserves reporting requirements. The systematic inconsistencies documented here raise questions about compliance with SEC, IFRS, and other regulatory frameworks.

Recommendations

This report recommends that Qatar implement the following measures to align its reserves reporting with international best practice:

  1. Classify reserves according to SPE-PRMS 2018: Clearly distinguish between OGIP, URG, 3P, 2P, and 1P reserves, with explicit disclosure of which category is being reported as "proved reserves"
  2. Publish annual reserves reconciliation: Disclose beginning balance, revisions, extensions, discoveries, production, and ending balance on an annual basis
  3. Correct condensate claims to physical constraints: Retract claims exceeding GIIP-c and disclose condensate reserves within the 18–29 Bn bbl range supported by CGR-adjusted MBAL analysis
  4. Commission independent third-party audit: Engage a qualified petroleum reserves evaluator to certify reserves according to SEC and SPE-PRMS standards
  5. Disclose economic and technical assumptions: Make public the price deck, discount rate, recovery factor, and other key assumptions underlying reserves estimates

Implementation of these recommendations would bring Qatar's reserves reporting into alignment with international standards and restore transparency to one of the world's most significant natural gas resources.

Glossary

Key Terms

OGIP
Original Gas In Place — the total volume of gas in a reservoir before any production
GIIP-c
Gas-Initial-In-Place for Condensate — the total volume of condensate in a gas-condensate reservoir
URG
Ultimate Recoverable Gas — the total volume expected to be recovered over the field's entire life, typically expressed as OGIP × recovery factor
1P / 2P / 3P
SPE-PRMS reserves categories: 1P (proved only), 2P (proved + probable), 3P (proved + probable + possible)
SEC 2P
Proved reserves as defined by SEC Regulation S-X Rule 4-10 — those quantities recoverable with "reasonable certainty" (P90)
MBAL
Material Balance Analysis — petroleum engineering technique using production and pressure data to estimate OGIP and reservoir performance
p/z
Pressure/Z-factor plot — a material balance technique where a linear trend indicates volumetric depletion
CGR
Condensate-Gas Ratio — volume of condensate per unit of gas, typically measured in barrels per million standard cubic feet (bbl/MMscf)
Recovery Factor (RF)
The percentage of OGIP or GIIP that can be economically recovered, typically 60–85% for gas reservoirs
FID
Final Investment Decision — the point at which a project is sanctioned and contingent resources convert to proved reserves under SEC guidelines
SPE-PRMS
Society of Petroleum Engineers Petroleum Resources Management System — international standard for reserves classification (current version: 2018)
Tcf
Trillion cubic feet — standard unit for large gas volumes (1 Tcf ≈ 28.3 billion cubic meters)
Bn bbl
Billion barrels — standard unit for liquid hydrocarbon volumes
